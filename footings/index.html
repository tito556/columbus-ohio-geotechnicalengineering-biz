Shallow Foundation Design in Columbus Ohio — Bearing Capacity and Settlement Control

Sound ground. Sound decisions.

LEARN MORE

The rig sets up over the footing location, a truck-mounted CME-75 driving a 2-inch split spoon through weathered till. In Columbus Ohio, we log refusal depth and SPT-N values every 2.5 feet — that log becomes the backbone of the shallow foundation design. We pull Shelby tubes from the cohesive layer above the till, seal them with wax, and run unconfined compression the same afternoon. The goal is a bearing pressure that keeps total settlement under 1 inch and differential under half that, per IBC Section 1805. For sites with deeper soft zones we often run CPT testing to catch thin clay lenses the split spoon misses, and we cross-check the shallow foundation design with grain-size data when the bearing stratum has more than 12 percent fines.

In Columbus glacial till, a 3-foot embedment keeps the bearing surface below the frost-active lean clay and typically delivers 3,000 psf net allowable — without overexcavation.

Process overview

Columbus sits on a stack of glacial deposits — mainly the Wisconsinan-age till that covers most of Franklin County. The till is dense, with N-values commonly above 25 below 8 feet, but it's capped by 3 to 6 feet of lean clay that swells with the freeze-thaw cycles. Our shallow foundation design accounts for that: we specify a minimum embedment of 36 inches to get below the active zone, and we calculate bearing capacity using the general shear equation with a factor of safety of 3. When the water table rises in spring — it can sit at 4 feet in the Olentangy floodplain — we switch to net allowable bearing and check for buoyancy effects on spread footings. The design package includes a settlement curve from elastic half-space theory and a bearing pressure chart stamped by our geotechnical engineer. For structures with heavy column loads we compare the shallow option against mat foundation analysis to confirm it's the more economical choice in the Columbus till profile.

Site-specific factors

The freeze-thaw cycle in central Ohio creates a problem that shallow foundation design in warmer climates doesn't face: the lean clay cap on Columbus till heaves when water freezes in the upper 30 inches. If the footing is too shallow, the heave lifts the perimeter and spring thaw leaves a gap under the center. We've seen differential movement of nearly an inch on foundations placed at 18 inches — cracking CMU walls in a single winter. The fix is straightforward: extend the footing down to 36 inches and backfill with granular material that drains. Another risk shows up near the Scioto and Olentangy river corridors, where a perched water table saturates the silty layer at 4 to 6 feet. That layer loses strength fast, and bearing capacity can drop below 2,000 psf if the contractor doesn't dewater during excavation. Our shallow foundation design report flags every boring where the water level was measured within 5 feet of the bearing elevation, and we add a construction-phase note requiring sump pumping until the concrete cures.

Common questions

How deep do footings need to be in Columbus Ohio?

Per IBC Table 1805.2.1 and local frost penetration data for Franklin County, the minimum footing depth is 36 inches below finished grade. That depth places the bearing surface below the lean clay cap that is susceptible to freeze-thaw heave. On sites where the glacial till is deeper — for example, near the Scioto River floodplain where alluvium can extend to 10 feet — we may specify a deeper embedment or a compacted granular fill replacement to reach competent bearing material.

What allowable bearing pressure can I expect on Columbus till?

For glacial till with SPT N-values above 25, we typically assign a net allowable bearing pressure of 3,000 psf, provided the total settlement check stays under 1 inch and differential settlement under 0.5 inch. This value is based on Meyerhof and Bowles SPT-N correlations with a factor of safety of 3. On smaller residential projects where the till is encountered at 3 to 4 feet, 2,500 psf is common to stay conservative on the settlement side.

How much does a shallow foundation design report cost in Columbus?

The cost for a shallow foundation design report in Columbus Ohio typically ranges from US$1,740 to US$3,470, depending on the number of borings, the depth of the investigation, and whether lab testing such as unconfined compression or grain-size analysis is required. A single-family residential report with two borings and a parameter sheet falls on the lower end; a commercial building with multiple column lines and settlement curves across the footprint moves toward the upper end.

What lab tests do you run to support shallow foundation design?

We run unconfined compression (ASTM D2166) on Shelby tube samples from the cohesive layer above the till, and grain-size analysis with hydrometer (ASTM D422/D6913) when the bearing stratum has more than 12 percent fines. Atterberg limits (ASTM D4318) are run on the near-surface lean clay to classify the frost-susceptible material. When the water table is high, we also run moisture content and dry density to back-calculate the degree of saturation for the bearing capacity equation.
