In-Situ Permeability Testing (Lefranc & Lugeon) in Columbus Ohio

Sound ground. Sound decisions.

LEARN MORE

Columbus sits at the seam of two distinct hydrogeological zones: the glacial till plains to the north and the deeply weathered shale bedrock of the Allegheny Plateau to the south. Where the Scioto River cuts through downtown, groundwater moves at rates that can either stabilize a foundation or compromise it entirely within a single wet season. The variable permeability of these deposits—from tight, overconsolidated clays to fractured Ohio Shale—means that laboratory permeameters alone cannot capture the true field behavior. Our in-situ testing program, executed with Lefranc and Lugeon procedures, provides the direct hydraulic conductivity values required for dewatering system design, cutoff wall verification, and seepage analysis under ASCE 7 and IBC standards. In many Columbus projects, we combine these measurements with a CPT investigation to correlate continuous soil behavior with discrete permeability zones, building a complete subsurface model before excavation begins.

A single Lugeon stage in fractured Ohio Shale tells you more about real seepage than a hundred lab permeability tests on intact core.

Methodology and scope

At an average elevation of 902 feet across Franklin County, Columbus experiences a water table that fluctuates sharply between spring recharge and late-summer drawdown. The Lefranc test, performed in boreholes at specific depths, measures the permeability of soil layers by recording the rate at which water infiltrates under a known hydraulic head: we run both constant-head and variable-head configurations depending on the expected conductivity range, typically between 10⁻⁵ and 10⁻³ cm/s for the silty clays common in the area. For rock mass characterization, the Lugeon test applies pressurized water in five-minute stages directly into packer-isolated intervals, quantifying the joint openness and fracture connectivity that govern flow through the Delaware and Columbus Limestones. Our field crews log flow rates and pressure responses every 60 seconds using calibrated digital manometers; the resulting Lugeon values allow the design team to decide whether curtain grouting is needed under the dam footprint or if natural drainage is sufficient. The test protocols follow ASTM D4630 for rock and the applicable sections of ASTM D5092 for soil–rock interfaces.

Site-specific factors

The Franklinton neighborhood, settled on the Scioto’s floodplain in 1797, has seen every major flood reshape its ground conditions, and the levees built after 1913 only partially control the subsurface flow paths. That history matters because any deep excavation or basement construction on the west side now encounters a complex layered system where sand lenses trapped between clay strata can carry water horizontally for hundreds of feet. If the permeability of those lenses is underestimated, dewatering systems fail: wells produce less than expected, excavation sidewalls slough, and soil piping initiates at the toe of the cut. In the Dublin and New Albany corridors, where retention ponds and stormwater infiltration basins are common, an overestimated permeability leads to standing water and regulatory non-compliance with Ohio EPA’s construction stormwater permit. The Lefranc and Lugeon tests eliminate this guesswork by measuring what the ground actually transmits, not what a textbook correlation predicts for a generic soil type.

Common questions

What is the difference between a Lefranc test and a Lugeon test, and when should each be used?

The Lefranc test measures hydraulic conductivity in soil by injecting or extracting water from an open borehole section and monitoring the flow rate under a known head difference. It works best in granular soils, silts, and clays where the formation can be assumed homogeneous over the test interval. The Lugeon test applies to rock: it uses a packer to isolate a section of the borehole, then injects water at increasing and decreasing pressure stages while recording the flow. The resulting Lugeon value reflects the combined effect of matrix porosity and fracture connectivity. In Columbus, we typically run Lefranc tests in the glacial drift and alluvial sands, and Lugeon tests in the limestone and shale bedrock.

How long does a field permeability test take on a typical Columbus site?

A single Lefranc test in soil usually requires 45 to 90 minutes of active testing after the borehole is advanced and the test section prepared, not counting setup and equipment calibration. A full five-stage Lugeon test in rock takes about 90 to 120 minutes per isolated interval, including packer inflation, pressure stabilization, and the stepped injection sequence. Deep boreholes with multiple test intervals can extend the program over two to three days. Weather delays are a factor in Columbus, particularly in spring when heavy rain can temporarily raise the water table and alter baseline conditions.

The cost for field permeability testing in the Columbus region typically ranges from US$550 to US$1,010 per test interval, depending on depth, access conditions, and whether a Lefranc or Lugeon configuration is required. We provide a fixed-price proposal after reviewing the geotechnical investigation plan and site logistics.

How do you interpret Lugeon values for grouting decisions in fractured limestone?

The interpretation follows the established Lugeon criterion: a value below 1 Lugeon indicates tight rock with minimal grout take, 1 to 3 Lugeon suggests low permeability where selective grouting may be considered, 3 to 10 Lugeon indicates moderately fractured conditions that generally require systematic grouting, and values above 10 Lugeon point to open fractures or karstic features needing intensive treatment. Our reports plot the full pressure-versus-flow curve for each stage and classify the flow regime—laminar, turbulent, dilation, or washout—as per Houlsby’s method, so the designer can predict grout consumption and select the appropriate mix and injection pressure.
